# Truthed User Interview Guide

## Pre-Interview Setup
- **Duration:** 30-45 minutes
- **Format:** Video call (record with permission) or in-person
- **Sample Size Goal:** 15-20 interviews across all segments

## Interview Opening (5 minutes)

**Introduction:**
"Hi [Name], thanks for taking the time to chat with me today. I'm working on a project related to how people consume and verify information online, and I'd love to learn about your experiences. There are no right or wrong answers - I'm just trying to understand how people like you navigate information online.

Before we start, is it okay if I record this conversation? It's just for my notes and won't be shared with anyone else."

**Warm-up Questions:**
- Tell me a bit about yourself and what you do
- How do you typically get your news and information? (social media, news sites, TV, etc.)
- About how much time would you say you spend reading news or information online each day?

## Core Problem Validation (15 minutes)

### Current Information Consumption Habits
- Walk me through your typical morning routine for catching up on news
- When you're scrolling through social media, how do you decide what to read or share?
- Have you ever come across information online that made you think "I'm not sure if this is true"?

### Misinformation Experiences
- Can you tell me about a time when you discovered that something you read or shared online wasn't accurate?
    - *Follow-up: How did you find out it was false?*
    - *Follow-up: How did that make you feel?*
- Have you ever held back from sharing something because you weren't sure if it was true?
- What types of information do you find yourself questioning most often? (health claims, political news, breaking news, etc.)

### Current Verification Behaviors
- When you're unsure about information, what do you do?
    - *Probe: Do you fact-check? How?*
    - *Probe: Do you ask others? Who?*
    - *Probe: Do you just ignore it?*
- What sources do you trust most for verifying information?
- Have you ever used fact-checking websites like Snopes or PolitiFact?
    - *If yes: What was your experience like?*
    - *If no: Why not?*

## Solution Validation (15 minutes)

### Privacy & Trust Concerns
- How do you feel about big tech companies (Facebook, Google, Twitter) deciding what information is true or false?
- What concerns, if any, do you have about your browsing data being collected?
- If a tool could help you identify questionable information, what would make you trust it?

### Feature Validation
**Present concept:** "I'm exploring a browser extension that would quietly analyze content as you browse and give you a small credibility score - like a traffic light system. It would explain why something might be questionable rather than just blocking it."

- What's your initial reaction to this idea?
- How would you want to be notified when content might be unreliable?
    - *Probe: Pop-up? Small icon? Color coding?*
- What information would you want to see when something is flagged?
    - *Probe: Source credibility? Fact-check links? Explanation of issues?*
- Would you rather have this work automatically, or only when you ask for it?

### Educational Approach
- Would you be interested in learning WHY something is potentially misleading, not just that it is?
- How detailed would you want these explanations to be?
- Would you find it valuable if the tool helped you become better at spotting misinformation yourself over time?

## Segment-Specific Questions (5-8 minutes)

### For Daily News Consumers:
- How often do you encounter breaking news that later turns out to be incomplete or inaccurate?
- What's your biggest frustration with staying informed online?
- How do you handle information overload?

### For Parents/Educators:
- Have you had to help kids/students navigate misinformation? Can you give me an example?
- What resources do you currently use to teach media literacy?
- How do you explain to young people how to evaluate information online?

### For Privacy-Conscious Users:
- What specific privacy practices do you follow online?
- What has made you lose trust in a service or platform in the past?
- How important is it that your data stays on your device vs. being processed in the cloud?

## Business Model Validation (3-5 minutes)

- If a tool like this existed and worked well, would you consider paying for it?
- What would you expect to get for free vs. what might be worth paying for?
- What would be a reasonable price for a premium version?
    - *Present options: $2.99, $4.99, $9.99 per month*
- Would you prefer a one-time purchase or monthly subscription?

## Wrap-up Questions (3 minutes)

- What would be the most important feature for you in a tool like this?
- What would make you decide NOT to use something like this?
- Is there anything I haven't asked about that you think is important?
- Do you know others who might be interested in chatting with me about this topic?

## Interview Logistics

### Recruiting Channels:
- Personal network and social media
- Local community groups (parents, educators)
- Privacy-focused online communities (Reddit: r/privacy, r/degoogle)
- Nextdoor for local parents/community members
- LinkedIn for professionals

### Success Metrics for Interviews:
- **Problem validation:** 70%+ of interviews confirm difficulty identifying misinformation
- **Solution validation:** 60%+ show interest in the proposed approach
- **Privacy validation:** 80%+ express concerns about current fact-checking solutions
- **Business validation:** 40%+ willing to pay for premium features

### Red Flags to Watch For:
- Participants who don't actually consume news online regularly
- Strong resistance to any form of content evaluation
- Expecting the tool to make decisions for them rather than inform them
- No current pain points with misinformation
